Exploring the Roots of Chronic Pain: Socioeconomic & Environmental Insights

Master’s Capstone • University at Albany (SUNY) • Dataset: World Health Survey (n = 54,019)

Last updated:

📌 Overview

This capstone quantifies how GDP per capita, civil conflict, education, age, and gender shape self-reported body pain using the World Health Survey (n=54,019) with OLS regression, logistic regression, and interaction models.

🎯 Research Objectives

  • Assess whether higher GDP per capita is associated with lower chronic pain.
  • Evaluate the impact of living in civil conflict zones on pain levels.
  • Determine how education, age, and gender influence pain prevalence.
  • Explore interaction effects between socioeconomic and demographic factors.

📊 Data & Methodology

  • Data Source: World Health Survey
  • Population: Individuals aged 20–60 from multiple countries
  • Variables: GDP per capita (log-transformed), civil conflict status (0/1), education level (ordinal), age, gender, body pain score (1–5)
  • Analytical Methods: OLS regression, logistic regression, interaction models

📈 Key Findings

  • Higher GDP per capita is linked to lower pain scores.
  • Living in conflict zones increases pain prevalence.
  • Higher education reduces reported pain.
  • Older individuals report higher pain levels.
  • Females report higher pain than males.
  • Education mitigates age-related pain increases; GDP buffers conflict’s impact on pain.

💡 Policy Implications

  • Expand healthcare access in low-GDP and conflict-affected regions.
  • Invest in education as a preventative public health measure.
  • Implement gender- and age-specific pain management programs.
