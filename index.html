Exploring the Roots of Chronic Pain: Socioeconomic & Environmental Insights

A mixed macro–micro analysis using the World Health Survey (n = 54,019) to quantify how GDP per capita, civil conflict, education, age, and gender shape self‑reported body pain and chronic pain risk. Methods include OLS, logistic regression, and interaction modeling.

Dataset: World Health Survey Methods: OLS, Logistic, Interactions Scope: 50k+ respondents Focus: Equity‑centered policy

Project Overview

Chronic pain undermines well‑being and productivity globally. Beyond biology, structural determinants like income, conflict exposure, and education stratify who experiences pain and who accesses relief. This project reframes chronic pain through a socio‑environmental lens, integrating country‑level capacity with individual‑level risk factors to explain variation in body‑pain intensity.

Research Questions

  • Do wealthier countries (higher GDP per capita) report lower average body pain?
  • Does living amid civil conflict elevate pain risk net of demographics?
  • How do education, age, and gender shape pain outcomes and interact?
Dependent variable: 1–5 body pain score (higher = worse)

Data & Methodology

Data: World Health Survey; individuals aged 20–60 with country tags. Key variables: log GDP per capita, civil conflict (0/1), education (ordinal), age, gender (female=1), body pain (1–5).

Models: OLS for continuous pain scores; logistic for binary chronic‑pain risk; interaction terms to test moderating effects (Age×Education, Conflict×GDP, Age×Gender). All models include controls and robust inference.

Statistical focus: effect sizes, standard errors, and joint significance across covariates; visualization via correlation heatmaps and grouped means.

Headline Results

  • GDP per capita ↓ pain: Higher GDP significantly predicts lower body pain (β < 0, p < .001).
  • Civil conflict ↑ pain: Conflict exposure associates with higher pain (p < .001).
  • Education ↓ pain: Higher educational attainment reduces pain (p < .001).
  • Age ↑ pain: Pain increases with age (p < .001).
  • Gender gap: Females report higher pain than males (p < .001).

Interaction Effects

  • Education × Age: Education attenuates age‑related increases in pain (negative interaction).
  • GDP × Conflict: Wealth partly buffers conflict’s pain burden (negative interaction).
  • Age × Gender: Pain rises faster with age for females.

Policy Implications

  • Scale equity‑focused pain services in low‑GDP and conflict‑affected settings.
  • Invest in health literacy & education as preventative infrastructure.
  • Adopt gender‑ and age‑sensitive pain management pathways.
